Biofouling ID Guide

Quick reference for divers and inspectors in Portugal - know what you're scraping off the hull.

Version 1.0 • Decree-Law 92/2019

What is this guide for?

The Job

You are going to clean hulls or inspect sea chests. You need to identify dangerous species before scraping them. This guide shows what to look for and what to report in national waters.

Why It Matters

Marine pests hitch rides on ship hulls. Some have already established themselves in national ports, threatening biodiversity and aquaculture. Divers are our first line of defense.

Priority Levels

  • INVASIVE Species listed in national legislation. Stop work and report to authorities within 24 hours.
  • WATCH Emerging threat or pest already established regionally. Document and report.
  • NATIVE Normal fouling in Portugal — no need to report.

Decree-Law no. 92/2019 of July 10.

⚠️ Suspect an Invasive Species?

  • STOP cleaning — halt work until advised by authorities.
  • Take photos - close up and wide shot (use your hand or a ruler for scale).
  • Note the location on the hull (depth, port/starboard, sea chest).
  • DO NOT return the species to the sea — keep an intact specimen if possible.
  • Report to ICNF or the SOS Environment hotline (SEPNA): 808 200 520.

Part 1: Invasive Hull Foulers (Primary Fouling)

These are the species that stick to the exterior of the hull and require mandatory reporting.

Japanese Wireweed
Sargassum muticum
INVASIVE
Marine Algae

Invasive brown algae that forms dense floating mats and attaches strongly to hard surfaces like hulls.

How to Spot It

  • Brown/yellowish algae
  • Small air vesicles (look like berries)
  • Can grow up to several meters long
  • Attaches to the hull via a strong basal disc
Wakame
Undaria pinnatifida
INVASIVE
Marine Algae

Marine algae listed as invasive in Portugal. Grows rapidly on hulls and port infrastructure.

How to Spot It

  • Large brown fronds
  • Distinct central vein (midrib)
  • Very wavy and frilly base (sporophyll)
Star Ascidian
Botryllus schlosseri
INVASIVE
Other Invertebrates

Colonial invertebrate that encrusts on hulls, creating gelatinous mats that smother other species.

How to Spot It

  • Colonies form star-shaped patterns
  • Gelatinous/rubbery texture
  • Various colors (orange, yellow, green, purple)
Striped Barnacle
Amphibalanus amphitrite
INVASIVE
Crustaceans

Warm-water crustacean. Causes huge hydrodynamic drag on ships and spreads rapidly.

How to Spot It

  • Calcareous cone shape
  • Vertical purple or brown stripes/lines
  • Direct primary fouling on metal/paint

Part 2: Niche Area Hitchhikers (Hidden)

These do not necessarily grow attached to the exterior hull. They hide in sea chests, suction grates, pipes, and bow thrusters.

Chinese Mitten Crab
Eriocheir sinensis
INVASIVE
Crustaceans

Invasive crustacean that often travels hidden in crevices or in ballast water. Migrates between the sea and freshwater estuaries.

How to Spot It

  • Claws covered in dense 'wool' or 'hair'
  • Olive-green to brown carapace
  • Four teeth on each side of the carapace
Soft-shell Clam
Mya arenaria[
INVASIVE
Molluscs

Bivalve mollusc species. Frequently found in sediments that accumulate at the bottom of sea chests of ships stationary for a long time.

How to Spot It

  • Oval and fragile (chalky) shell
  • A long fleshy siphon (tube) that often does not retract fully
  • Hides in accumulated mud and sludge

Part 3: Native Species (The Normal Stuff)

This is what you will find most of the time in Portuguese waters. Knowing the native species helps immediately detect when something different (invasive) appears.

Mediterranean Mussel
Mytilus galloprovincialis
Native Molluscs

Our common mussel. Creates dense beds on hulls and port pillars.

How to Spot It

  • Blue-black or brownish shell
  • Teardrop/pointed triangle shape
  • Does not have bright green tones
